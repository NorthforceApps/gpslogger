Privacy guide

Why offline GPS logging matters for privacy

Location history is sensitive. A private route journal should make recording useful without turning every walk, drive or trip into a cloud profile.

Quick answer

Local-first GPS logging reduces exposure because saved routes stay on your phone by default. GPS Logger only sends location data when you choose a feature that needs it, such as App Cloud live sharing, export to another app, self-hosted live upload, map services, or terrain downloads for elevation correction.

Local-first route history lowers exposure

GPS Logger stores recorded tracks on your device. That means your saved walking routes, hiking tracks, bike commutes and road trips are not uploaded as a location trail by default.

This matters because route history reveals habits: home and work areas, school runs, repeated walks, travel dates and quiet routines. Keeping the archive local by default gives you a smaller privacy footprint than a system where every saved trip becomes part of a cloud account.

Background location should be visible and controlled

Android background location lets a GPS recording continue while screen is off. GPS Logger records only when you start it, with an ongoing notification during tracking. Optional auto-start after reboot exists, but you control it.

A visible notification is part of the privacy model. You should be able to tell when an app is recording or sharing location. Hidden tracking is not the goal; complete user-started route logs are.

Live sharing should be temporary

Sometimes sharing current location is useful. GPS Logger App Cloud live share sends current position to admitted users with your resettable code and follower limit. It does not store a server-side trail; current position is overwritten and removed after stopping.

Exports should be open

Private does not mean trapped. GPX and KML export let you move route data into mapping tools, backups or other apps when you decide. See how to record and export GPX tracks on Android.

Private does not mean pretending the internet is never used

A privacy-first GPS app still has real dependencies. GPS Logger uses Google Maps to display maps, Google Play for purchases, Firebase services for optional App Cloud live sharing and optional diagnostics, and online terrain data for elevation correction features. The important distinction is whether saved route history is uploaded as a location trail by default. In GPS Logger, it is not.

Good privacy questions before choosing a GPS logger

  • Can I record without an account?
  • Where are saved tracks stored by default?
  • Can I export standard GPX or KML files?
  • Does live sharing store a route history or only current position?
  • Can I reset access and delete local history?
  • Does the app explain background location clearly?

Privacy checklist for route tracking

  • Use recording only when needed.
  • Keep routes local unless sharing or exporting has a purpose.
  • Reset live-share code when access should end.
  • Export backups to storage you trust.
  • Read app privacy policy before long-term use.

FAQ

Is GPS Logger offline-only?

No. Saved tracks are local by default, but maps, optional App Cloud live sharing, purchases, diagnostics and elevation features can use online services.

Does recording require an account?

No. Recording, viewing, importing and exporting tracks do not require an account.

Does live sharing expose my old tracks?

No. App Cloud live sharing relays current position while sharing is active. It does not upload your saved route history.

Can I leave the app later with my data?

Yes. Export individual tracks as GPX/KML or make a full-history GPX ZIP backup.
